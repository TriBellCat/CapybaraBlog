Features

Capybaras are distinguished by their heavy, barrel-shaped bodies and short heads. Their fur is reddish-brown on the upper parts, transitioning to yellowish-brown beneath. These large rodents have blunt snouts, short legs, small ears, and almost no tail. Despite their size, capybaras have a rather endearing appearance, with a calm and friendly expression that reflects their gentle disposition.


Relatives

Capybaras are part of a family of rodents known as Caviidae, which includes several other species. Their closest living relatives are the lesser capybara, guinea pigs, and the rock cavies. They are also related to guinea pigs and more distantly to the agouti, chinchilla, and nutria. These relatives share a common South American heritage, with adaptations that allow them to thrive in varied environments, from dense forests to open savannas. Habitats

Capybaras are native to several countries across South America. To be more exact, there are found in Brazil,Colombia, Venezuela, Bolivia, Peru, Ecuador, Argentina, Paraguay, and Uruguay. These countries provide the warm climates and water-rich environments that capybaras thrive in.

Interestingly, while not native, there have also been sightings of capybaras in Florida and they are a beloved animal in Japan.

They are also found near waterbeds such as rivers, lakes, ponds, marshes, and swamps.
